## What are personas?

Much like any other design discipline, understanding your audience is a critical element of designing right. Personas  describe the most important information about who your technology is for. 

A persona is a named, semi-fictional representation of a type of person who interacts with your project. Personas are not real individuals — they are composite portraits synthesized from real data, used as stable reference points when making decisions about documentation, design, governance, and community infrastructure.

The concept was formalized in interaction design by Alan Cooper in the 1990s as a tool for keeping software teams oriented toward actual user needs rather than internal assumptions. Since then, personas have migrated into technical writing, UX research, product strategy, organizational design, and open source community management.

A well-constructed persona answers questions like:

*  What does this person already know when they arrive at our project?
*  What are they trying to accomplish, and why?
*  What are the friction points most likely to stop them?
*  What language do they use for the concepts we're working with?
*  What prior commitments — technical, political, philosophical — do they carry?

What a Persona Is Not

* Demographics (Age, Sex, etc...)
* A real person (do not build personas around specific individuals)
* A stereotype (a good persona contains internal tensions and specificity)

## Persona template

### Persona N: [Given Name], the [Archetype Label]

**Fictional name:** [First Last]
**Archetype sources:** [Biographical figures or texts that informed this persona]

**Context:**
[2–4 sentences describing who this person is, where they come from, and how they relate to your project.]

**Goals:**
[What is this person trying to accomplish through your project? What success looks like for them.]

**Prior knowledge:**
[What do they already know? Be specific about domains and levels — not just "technical" or "non-technical."]

**Mental models:**
[How do they think about the problem space? What frameworks, metaphors, or heuristics do they use?]

**Constraints:**
[What limits their engagement? Time, tools, connectivity, trust, capital, context?]

**Characteristic quote:**
*"[A sentence this person might actually say, capturing their orientation in their own voice.]*"

**Documentation implications:**
[Concrete, actionable guidance for documentation decisions based on this persona's needs.]
